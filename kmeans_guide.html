Topic 1

Problem Setup & Intuition

K-Means groups unlabeled points so that points in the same group are similar to each other and different from points in other groups. There's no target y — it's unsupervised: we're discovering structure, not predicting a known label.

Core intuition: given N points, if you had to summarize them with just K representative points (centroids), where would you place those centroids to minimize total distortion? Assign every point to its nearest centroid, then move each centroid to the center of mass of its assigned points. Repeat until stable.

💡

Think of dropping K pins on a map — every city joins its nearest pin, then each pin relocates to the center of its cities. Eventually the pins stop moving — that's convergence.

Real-world uses

  • Customer segmentation — group users by behavior with no existing labels
  • Image compression — reduce millions of colors to K representative colors
  • Anomaly detection — points far from any centroid flagged as outliers
  • Feature engineering — cluster ID used as a categorical feature for downstream models
Key Structural Assumption

K-Means assumes clusters are roughly spherical, similarly sized, and similarly dense — because it uses Euclidean distance to a single centroid as the sole assignment criterion. This one assumption is the root cause of most of its limitations (Topic 8).

Why K-Means fails on non-convex shapes

K-Means always produces straight-line (Voronoi) decision boundaries between centroids, since every point is assigned purely by nearest-centroid distance. On classic toy datasets like make_circles (concentric circles) or make_moons (interleaving crescents), the true separating boundary is curved — a straight line can only slice the shape into pie-wedges, mixing points from both true groups into every resulting cluster.

Quick Check — Intuition
Q. Why do concentric circles defeat K-Means even though the true clusters are visually obvious?
A. Both circles can share nearly the same center of mass — so distance-to-centroid can't distinguish inner ring from outer ring; every point at a given radius is equidistant from the shared center regardless of which ring it belongs to.
Interview Trap

Answering "when would you not use K-Means?" with just "weird shapes" — the precise answer is: whenever clusters aren't linearly separable by centroid distance (non-convex, unequal density/size).

Topic 2

Objective Function (WCSS / Inertia)

K-Means needs a single number quantifying "how good is this clustering" so it can minimize it. That number is Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS), a.k.a. inertia — the total squared distance of every point from its own cluster's centroid.

J = Σᵢ₌₁ᴷ Σ_{x∈Cᵢ} ‖x − μᵢ‖²

K = number of clusters  ·  Cᵢ = set of points assigned to cluster i  ·  x = a point in Cᵢ  ·  μᵢ = centroid (mean) of cluster i  ·  ‖x−μᵢ‖² = squared Euclidean distance from point to its own centroid.

We square the distance for two reasons: it penalizes far points more heavily (quadratic penalty), and it makes J differentiable, giving a clean closed-form update — the mean is provably the point that minimizes sum of squared distances (∂J/∂μ=0 → μ = mean).

Worked example

1D cluster, points {2, 4, 6}. True mean μ=4: J = (2−4)² + (4−4)² + (6−4)² = 4+0+4 = 8. Using a wrong center μ=3 instead: J = 1+1+9 = 11 — worse, confirming the mean is optimal.

Interpretation

Lower J = tighter clusters. But J trivially → 0 as K→N (every point its own cluster) — so J alone can never tell you the "right" K; that's exactly why the elbow method (Topic 6) exists.

What if we used absolute distance instead?

Minimizing Σ|x−μ| instead of Σ(x−μ)² makes the median optimal, not the mean — because the derivative of |x−μ| is a constant ±1, and setting the sum of signs to zero requires an equal count of points above/below μ (the definition of median). This is the K-Medians variant — more outlier-robust, no closed-form, needs iterative search.

LossOptimal centerRobust to outliers?
L2 (squared)MeanNo
L1 (absolute)MedianYes
Quick Check — Objective Function
Q. Why can't inertia alone be used to select K?
A. It monotonically decreases (or stays flat) as K increases, hitting 0 when K=N — always "improving" with more clusters regardless of whether the extra split is meaningful.
Topic 3

The Algorithm (Lloyd's Algorithm)

Minimizing J directly is a combinatorial, NP-hard problem — checking all possible point-to-cluster assignments doesn't scale. Lloyd's algorithm alternates two easy sub-problems instead of solving the whole thing at once — a classic coordinate descent pattern.

1. Initialize K centroids μ₁, ..., μ_K 2. Repeat until convergence: a. Assignment: for each point x, assign to cluster i = argmin_i ||x - μ_i||² b. Update: for each cluster i, recompute μ_i = mean of all points assigned to it 3. Stop when assignments stop changing

Each step is individually exactly optimal for its half of the problem — assignment can't do better than nearest-centroid for fixed centroids, and update can't do better than the mean for fixed assignments (Topic 2). So J never increases.

Worked example — 1D points {1, 2, 9, 10}, K=2

Bad init: μ₁=1, μ₂=2. Iteration 1 assigns C₁={1}, C₂={2,9,10} → update gives μ₁=1, μ₂=7. Iteration 2 reassigns C₁={1,2}, C₂={9,10} → update gives μ₁=1.5, μ₂=9.5. Iteration 3 reassigns to the same clusters — converged, correctly, despite the bad start.

Convergence Proof Sketch

Assignment step: for frozen centroids, nearest-centroid assignment is provably optimal per point. Update step: for frozen assignments, the mean provably minimizes squared distance (Topic 2). Both steps solve their half exactly, so J is monotonically non-increasing — bounded below by 0 → must converge. But it converges to a local optimum only, since assignments and centroids are never jointly searched.

Local optimum — a genuine stuck case

True groups A={1,2,3,4}, B={5,6}, C={20,21}, K=3. Bad init μ={1,2,20} (two centroids inside A). After iterating, it converges to a stable but wrong partition: C₁={1,2}, C₂={3,4,5,6}, C₃={20,21} — group A gets split down the middle and its back half silently merges with all of B. No point wants to flip anymore (stable), yet this isn't the natural grouping a human would draw. WCSS is locally minimal, globally suboptimal.

💡

K-Means is a special case of the EM algorithm: assignment step ≈ E-step (hard instead of soft), update step ≈ M-step (mean instead of probability-weighted mean). GMM is the general, soft-assignment version.

Interview Trap

Assuming any "bad-looking" init gets stuck. Two well-separated groups with K=2 almost always self-correct within a couple of iterations — genuine local optima need enough clusters (K≥3) that centroids can get trapped competing over one natural group while another gets wrongly merged.

Topic 4

Distance Metrics

d(x, μ)² = Σⱼ₌₁ᴰ (xⱼ − μⱼ)²

The centroid update (mean = optimal center) is only mathematically guaranteed to minimize squared Euclidean distance — Euclidean distance and K-Means are mathematically coupled, not an arbitrary default. Swap the metric and the mean is no longer provably optimal.

Worked example — why mean fails under Manhattan distance

Points {1, 2, 100}: mean=34.3 gives Σ|x−34.3| = 131.3; median=2 gives Σ|x−2| = 99 — lower. Mean is not optimal under L1; you need K-Medians (median-based update) instead of naively plugging L1 into vanilla K-Means.

MetricFormulaUse caseCompatible update rule
Euclidean (L2)√Σ(xⱼ−μⱼ)²Default, continuous features✅ Mean
Manhattan (L1)Σ|xⱼ−μⱼ|Outlier-robust, grid data❌ Needs median → K-Medians
Cosine1 − (x·μ)/(‖x‖‖μ‖)Text/embeddings, direction matters❌ Needs normalization trick

The normalization trick for cosine K-Means

For unit vectors (‖x‖=‖μ‖=1): ‖x−μ‖² = ‖x‖²+‖μ‖²−2(x·μ) = 2−2(x·μ) = 2(1−cosθ) — squared Euclidean distance between unit vectors is just 2× cosine distance. Since this is a constant scaling, the argmin ranking is identical — so L2-normalizing vectors then running standard Euclidean K-Means gives the exact same cluster assignments as true cosine K-Means, while keeping the mean-based update mathematically valid.

Key Insight

For text embeddings/document clustering: always L2-normalize vectors before running standard K-Means — this quietly converts your Euclidean K-Means into cosine-based clustering without breaking convergence guarantees.

Interview Trap

Assuming you can just swap the distance function in the assignment step (e.g. to cosine) while keeping the mean-based update — this breaks the monotonic-decrease guarantee, since the update step is no longer solving the correct sub-optimization for that metric.

Topic 5

Centroid Initialization (Random vs. K-Means++)

Bad initialization can permanently trap Lloyd's algorithm in a wrong-but-stable local optimum (Topic 3). Initialization is purely an engineering fix — it doesn't change the convergence guarantee, only the odds of landing in a good local optimum.

Random init

Pick K data points uniformly at random. Nothing stops two centroids from landing in the same natural cluster. Standard mitigation: run multiple times (n_init), keep the lowest final J — brute-force hedging, not a structural fix.

K-Means++ (the structural fix)

1. Pick 1st centroid uniformly at random 2. For each remaining point x, compute D(x) = squared distance to NEAREST chosen centroid 3. Pick next centroid with probability proportional to D(x)² 4. Repeat 2-3 until K centroids chosen 5. Run standard Lloyd's algorithm from there

Worked example — points {1,2,3,20,21}, K=2

μ₁=2 picked first. D(x) values: 1,0,1,324,361 (sum=687). P(x=20)≈47%, P(x=21)≈53% — the far group has ~99.7% combined chance of supplying the 2nd centroid, vs. only 20% each under pure random init.

Random InitK-Means++
Risk of same-cluster centroidsHighLow
Deterministic?NoNo — still probabilistic
Guarantees global optimum?NoNo — just better odds
Why probabilistic, not deterministic-farthest?

If you always deterministically picked the single farthest point, a single noisy outlier would always be chosen as a centroid, every run, with 100% certainty — permanently wasting a cluster slot on garbage. Weighted-random selection still favors the outlier strongly but not with certainty, so across multiple n_init restarts there's a real chance of recovering the correct structure instead of the same bad result every time.

Quick Check — Initialization
Q. Does k-means++ guarantee the global optimum?
A. No — it only makes bad local optima much less likely, probabilistically. `n_init` restarts remain standard practice even with k-means++.
Topic 6

Choosing K

Elbow Method

Plot inertia J vs. K. J always decreases, but the rate of decrease drops sharply after the true cluster count, then flattens — that bend is the "elbow." Splitting a genuine cluster removes lots of distortion (steep drop); subdividing an already-tight cluster barely helps (shallow drop).

Elbow at K=3 — the K=3→4 drop (10) is tiny compared to K=2→3 (180). Limitation: real data often has no visually sharp bend, making this subjective.

Silhouette Score

s(i) = (b(i) − a(i)) / max(a(i), b(i))

a(i) = avg distance from point i to others in its own cluster (cohesion, want low)  ·  b(i) = avg distance to the nearest neighboring cluster (separation, want high). Range: −1 to +1; ~+1 = well inside own cluster, ~0 = boundary/ambiguous, negative = likely misassigned.

Worked example: a(i)=2, b(i)=5 → s(i) = (5−2)/max(2,5) = 3/5 = 0.6 — solidly good. Average s(i) across all points, per K, and pick the K that maximizes it — unlike inertia, over-splitting hurts this score because sub-clusters end up close together, dragging b(i) down.

Gap Statistic

Gap(K) = E[log(J_random)] − log(J_data)

Compares real data's inertia against inertia from uniformly random reference data at the same K. Large gap → real structure exists at this K. More statistically rigorous than elbow, but computationally expensive (needs multiple random reference samples).

Key Insight

Inertia only measures within-cluster cohesion — it mechanically improves with more K regardless of meaning. Silhouette forces a trade-off by also weighing inter-cluster separation, so it peaks at a genuinely good K instead of monotonically improving.

Interview Trap

Trusting an ambiguous elbow plot over a sharp silhouette peak. When they disagree, silhouette's explicit quantitative trade-off is the more reliable signal.

Topic 7

Convergence & Local Optima

J decreases monotonically each iteration (Topic 3) and is bounded below by 0 → guaranteed convergence to some value. But finding the truly optimal clustering is NP-hard: the number of ways to partition N points into K groups is a Stirling number of the second kind, S(N,K), growing roughly as K^N/K! — combinatorially explosive. Lloyd's algorithm greedily descends to the nearest local minimum instead of exhaustively searching this space.

💡

Tiny example: N=3 points {a,b,c}, K=2 → S(3,2)=3 possible partitions: {a}{b,c}, {b}{a,c}, {c}{a,b}. Even this explodes fast for real N — exact global search is infeasible for anything beyond toy sizes.

Why the algorithm can never cycle forever

Suppose (for contradiction) it cycled through partitions P₁→P₂→...→P₁. Since J strictly decreases whenever the partition genuinely changes, this implies J(P₁) > J(P₁) — a contradiction. So the algorithm can never revisit an earlier partition unless it has truly stopped changing. Combined with only finitely many partitions existing, it must hit a partition it can't leave (converge) within finite steps — a pigeonhole + strict-monotonicity argument.

QuestionAnswer
Always converges?Yes — guaranteed, finite steps
Always same result?No — random init (even in k-means++'s first pick) varies runs
Reaches global optimum?No — only a local optimum

Practical mitigations

  • K-Means++ initialization — reduces (doesn't eliminate) bad local optima
  • Multiple restarts (n_init) — keep the run with lowest final J
  • Compare final J across runs as a diagnostic for suspiciously poor convergence
  • Fix random_state for reproducibility
Interview Trap

"Is K-Means guaranteed to terminate?" — Yes, always, in finite steps. This is separate from "does it find the best answer?" — No. Don't conflate termination with optimality.

Equal size, different density — a distinct failure mode

Cluster A: 100 points packed tightly in [0,1]. Cluster B: 100 points (equal size!) spread across [10,30]. K-Means punishes B's natural spread as if it "should" be tighter — since J's squared-distance penalty has no concept of a cluster being allowed a larger radius by nature — sometimes carving an unnecessary extra centroid into B even though it's genuinely one cluster.

💡

Size affects the update step (which cluster's mean dominates); density affects the assignment step (whether "far from centroid" truly means "wrong cluster"). They are genuinely distinct failure modes, not restatements of each other.

Interview Trap

Listing only "non-convex shapes" when asked for K-Means limitations. A complete answer covers all six: shape, size, density, scale, outliers, and fixed-K.

Topic 9

Feature Scaling

Standardization: x' = (x − μ) / σ

Standardization sets every feature's mean to 0 and standard deviation to 1. This forces every feature's typical spread to be numerically equal — not because values become bounded to a fixed range, but because dividing by σ literally makes spread=1 for every feature, so a "1 std swing" in any feature contributes equally to squared distance regardless of original units.

Worked example — before/after scaling changes the actual clustering

Points (age, income): A=(25,40000), B=(65,42000), C=(45,90000). Unscaled: d(A,B)²=1600+4,000,000≈4.0M; d(A,C)²=400+2.5B. A and B look "closer" purely because income differs by only 2000 — age (25 vs 65) is essentially invisible. After standardizing, age would properly contribute.

Min-Max: x' = (x − x_min) / (x_max − x_min)

Squeezes to [0,1] but is more outlier-sensitive — one extreme value stretches x_max/x_min, compressing all "normal" values into a tiny sub-range. Standardization is generally preferred for K-Means.

Common Misconception

Scaling is not a minor preprocessing nicety — skipping it can produce a structurally different, meaningfully wrong clustering, not just a slightly-off one.

Categorical features

One-hot encoded columns are bounded but still interact oddly with Euclidean distance (treats all category differences as equally dissimilar). K-Means is a poor fit for pure categorical data — use K-Modes (Hamming distance + mode) or K-Prototypes for mixed data.

Quick Check — Feature Scaling
Q. Why is bounding to [0,1] not actually necessary to fix the domination problem?
A. Domination is purely about relative spread/variance between features — standardization equalizes spread (std=1 for every feature) directly, without needing a hard bounded range.
Topic 10

Variants: K-Medoids, Mini-Batch K-Means, GMM

K-Medoids (PAM)

Cluster "center" must be an actual data point (medoid) — the one minimizing total dissimilarity to its cluster. Update step tests every possible swap: O(n²) per cluster per iteration — far more expensive than K-Means' O(n) mean, so it doesn't scale, but it's inherently more outlier-robust since an extreme point rarely minimizes total distance.

Mini-Batch K-Means

Recomputes centroids using small random batches instead of the full dataset each iteration — same speed/accuracy trade-off philosophy as SGD vs. full-batch gradient descent. Much faster, scales to millions of rows, converges to a slightly worse final J.

GMM (Gaussian Mixture Model)

Soft, probabilistic membership across all K clusters instead of hard assignment. Each cluster gets a mean μ_k, a covariance matrix Σ_k (allows elongated/tilted/differently-sized clusters), and a mixing weight π_k. E-step computes soft membership probabilities; M-step updates μ_k, Σ_k, π_k using those probabilities as weights.

K-Means = constrained GMM

Force Σ_k = σ²I (spherical, identical covariance for every cluster) → the weighted distance (x−μ_k)ᵀΣ_k⁻¹(x−μ_k) collapses to plain scaled Euclidean distance, identical for every cluster. Force E-step probabilities to hard 0/1 → every point belongs 100% to one cluster. Apply both constraints and GMM's E-step/M-step become exactly Lloyd's assignment/update steps. K-Means is GMM restricted to spherical/equal covariance + hard assignments — every bit of GMM's extra expressiveness comes from giving up exactly these two things.

Saying "GMM is just a probabilistic K-Means" without being able to name the exact two constraints (spherical/equal covariance + hard assignment) that connect them mathematically.

Topic 11

Complexity Analysis

Per iteration: O(N × K × D) · Total: O(N × K × D × I)

Assignment step dominates: N points × K centroids × D dimensions per distance computation. Update step is O(N×D) — cheaper, since each point is added once to its cluster's running sum. Space: O(N×D + K×D), dominated by storing the dataset itself.

Worked example

N=1,000,000, K=10, D=50, I=20 → O(10¹⁰) operations ≈ seconds on modern hardware. Compare to K-Medoids' O(N²D) per iteration at the same N — O(10¹²) per iteration, utterly infeasible. This is exactly why K-Medoids doesn't scale and Lloyd's K-Means does.

Common Misconception

K-Means isn't "slow because it's iterative" — each iteration is linear in N, and the iteration count I stays small (10-50) and roughly constant regardless of N in practice.

High-dimensional data (e.g. D=10,000 text embeddings)

When N and D are both large, they jointly dominate the O(NKD) cost. Standard fix: reduce D first via PCA (or Truncated SVD for sparse data like TF-IDF, since it skips centering and preserves sparsity). This fixes both the speed bottleneck and the curse-of-dimensionality effect where distances stop being discriminative at very high D.

Interview Trap

Reducing D only for speed, missing the second reason: high-D Euclidean distances become less meaningful as all pairwise distances converge toward similar values (curse of dimensionality) — dimensionality reduction also improves clustering quality, not just runtime.

Topic 12

Cluster Validation — Visual + Metrics

Visual validation

Direct 2D/3D scatter with centroids marked for low-D data. For high-D: PCA projection (linear, fast, cheap sanity check) or t-SNE/UMAP (non-linear, preserves local neighborhood structure — more visually faithful, but distorts global distances).

Common Misconception

t-SNE plots are not ground truth for "how separated are clusters really" — cluster sizes and inter-cluster distances in a t-SNE plot are not meaningful; only whether points land in the same visual blob is meaningful.

Signs of bad clustering visually: heavily overlapping colors with no clear boundary; one giant cluster plus several 1-2 point clusters (outlier-driven distortion); a centroid sitting visually outside all its own assigned points (instability from bad init or non-convex data).

Davies-Bouldin Index

DB = (1/K) Σᵢ max_{j≠i} [ (sᵢ + sⱼ) / d(cᵢ, cⱼ) ]

sᵢ = avg distance of cluster i's points to its own centroid  ·  d(cᵢ,cⱼ) = distance between centroids i and j. Uses the worst-case (most confusable) neighboring cluster per i — lower DB = better.

Worked example: s₁=2, s₂=3, d(c₁,c₂)=10 → DB = ½[(2+3)/10 + (3+2)/10] = ½(0.5+0.5) = 0.5 — good, tight and well-separated.

💡

Why max, not average, over neighbors? A clustering is only as good as its weakest link. Averaging over 9 well-separated neighbors and 1 badly-overlapping one dilutes the real defect (e.g. avg≈0.38 looks fine while max=3.0 correctly flags the problem) — max is deliberately pessimistic, the same philosophy behind reporting P99 latency instead of average latency.

Calinski-Harabasz Index

CH = [Between-cluster variance / (K−1)] / [Within-cluster variance / (N−K)]

An F-statistic-style ratio — higher CH = better. Much cheaper to compute than silhouette (cluster-level sums, not O(N²) pairwise) — practical for very large N.

MetricDirectionNeeds labels?Cost
SilhouetteHigher better (−1 to 1)NoO(N²) — slow
Davies-BouldinLower betterNoFast

ARI/NMI compare predicted labels against true labels (corrected for chance agreement) — rarely available in real unsupervised settings, but standard for benchmarking a clustering algorithm against known labeled datasets (e.g. Iris, MNIST).

Interview Trap

Treating Davies-Bouldin's averaging-vs-max choice as arbitrary. It's deliberate: validation metrics that exist to catch failure modes should aggregate pessimistically (worst case), not optimistically (average).

Topic 13

Implementation from Scratch (NumPy)

A from-scratch implementation ties every earlier concept into working code: k-means++ init (Topic 5), vectorized assignment (Topic 3/11), mean-based update (Topic 2), and tolerance-based convergence (Topic 7).

import numpy as np class KMeansScratch: def __init__(self, k=3, max_iters=100, tol=1e-4, init='kmeans++', random_state=None): self.k = k self.max_iters = max_iters self.tol = tol self.init = init self.rng = np.random.default_rng(random_state) def _init_centroids(self, X): n_samples = X.shape[0] if self.init == 'random': idx = self.rng.choice(n_samples, self.k, replace=False) return X[idx].copy() # k-means++: weight next pick by squared distance to nearest chosen centroid centroids = np.empty((self.k, X.shape[1])) first_idx = self.rng.integers(n_samples) centroids[0] = X[first_idx] for i in range(1, self.k): dist_sq = np.min( np.sum((X[:, None, :] - centroids[None, :i, :]) ** 2, axis=2), axis=1 ) probs = dist_sq / dist_sq.sum() next_idx = self.rng.choice(n_samples, p=probs) centroids[i] = X[next_idx] return centroids def fit(self, X): self.centroids = self._init_centroids(X) for iteration in range(self.max_iters): # Assignment step: O(N*K*D) via broadcasting distances = np.sum( (X[:, None, :] - self.centroids[None, :, :]) ** 2, axis=2 ) labels = np.argmin(distances, axis=1) # Update step: mean minimizes squared distance new_centroids = np.array([ X[labels == j].mean(axis=0) if np.any(labels == j) else self.centroids[j] for j in range(self.k) ]) shift = np.linalg.norm(new_centroids - self.centroids) self.centroids = new_centroids if shift < self.tol: break self.labels_ = labels self.n_iter_ = iteration + 1 self.inertia_ = self._compute_inertia(X, labels) return self def _compute_inertia(self, X, labels): return sum( np.sum((X[labels == j] - self.centroids[j]) ** 2) for j in range(self.k) ) def predict(self, X): distances = np.sum( (X[:, None, :] - self.centroids[None, :, :]) ** 2, axis=2 ) return np.argmin(distances, axis=1)

Broadcasting mechanics

X[:, None, :] has shape (N,1,D); centroids[None, :, :] has shape (1,K,D). Broadcasting stretches both to (N,K,D), so result[n,k,d] = difference between point n's d-th coordinate and centroid k's d-th coordinate — every pairwise difference computed at once. Summing over axis=2 (D) collapses this to the final (N,K) distance matrix.

Empty Cluster Edge Case

If an unlucky assignment leaves a cluster with zero points, .mean() on an empty array produces NaN, silently corrupting all future iterations. Standard fixes: keep the previous centroid unchanged, or re-seed it as the point farthest from all current centroids (mini k-means++ style recovery).

Production note: the 3D broadcast intermediate array costs O(NKD) memory temporarily. Libraries like sklearn instead expand ‖x−μ‖² = ‖x‖²+‖μ‖²−2x·μ and use matrix multiplication — more memory-efficient at scale.

Interview Trap

"What happens if a cluster becomes empty during training?" — a real, commonly-asked edge case. Forgetting to guard against it is a common from-scratch implementation bug.

Topic 14

  • t-SNE plots over-interpreted as true global cluster geometry
Interview Trap

Conflating "converges" with "converges well." K-Means always terminates in finite steps — that guarantee says nothing about clustering quality.

Practice

30 Interview Questions

Q1Easy
What is the objective function K-Means minimizes?
+

Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (inertia): J = Σᵢ Σ_{x∈Cᵢ} ‖x−μᵢ‖² — total squared distance of every point to its own cluster's centroid.

Q2Easy
Is K-Means supervised or unsupervised, and why?
+

Unsupervised — there is no target variable y; the algorithm discovers group structure purely from feature similarity.

Q3Easy
What are the two steps of Lloyd's algorithm?
+

Assignment (assign each point to its nearest centroid) and Update (recompute each centroid as the mean of its assigned points), repeated until assignments stop changing.

Q4Easy
Why must you specify K upfront in K-Means?
+

The algorithm has no internal mechanism to discover the number of clusters — it only optimizes centroid positions and assignments for a fixed, given K.

Q5Easy
What does it mean when inertia is 0?
+

Every point is its own cluster (K=N) — a trivial, useless clustering, since inertia mechanically shrinks to 0 as K approaches N.

Q6Easy
Why is feature scaling important before running K-Means?
+

Euclidean distance combines all features into a single number; a feature with a much larger numeric range dominates the distance and effectively drowns out smaller-scale features, distorting the clustering.

Q7Easy
Is K-Means deterministic?
+

No — random initialization (even k-means++'s first pick) means results vary run to run unless random_state is fixed.

Q8Easy
What's the role of a centroid in K-Means?
+

It's the representative point (mean) of a cluster — every point is assigned based on distance to the nearest centroid, and centroids are recomputed each iteration from their assigned points.

Q9Easy
Name two real-world applications of K-Means.
+

Customer segmentation (grouping users by behavior without labels) and image compression (reducing millions of colors to K representative colors). Anomaly detection and feature engineering (cluster ID as a feature) are also common.

Q10Easy
What distance metric does standard K-Means use, and why?
+

Squared Euclidean distance — because it's the only metric for which the update step's mean is provably optimal (minimizes total squared distance).

Q11Medium
Prove that the mean minimizes the sum of squared distances within a cluster.
+

Taking ∂J/∂μ = ∂/∂μ Σ(x−μ)² = −2Σ(x−μ), setting it to zero gives Σx = nμ, so μ = (1/n)Σx — the arithmetic mean. This is a global minimum since J is convex in μ.

Q12Medium
Why does K-Means fail on the classic concentric-circles (make_circles) dataset?
+

Both the inner circle and outer ring share nearly the same center of mass, so distance-to-centroid can't separate them — K-Means only produces straight-line (Voronoi) boundaries, but separating these clusters requires a curved boundary.

Q13Medium
Explain how k-means++ initialization works and why it reduces bad local optima.
+

The first centroid is picked randomly; each subsequent centroid is picked with probability proportional to its squared distance from the nearest already-chosen centroid. This actively spreads centroids across distinct regions of the data instead of letting them cluster together, sharply reducing the chance two centroids land in the same natural group.

Q14Medium
Why is k-means++ probabilistic rather than deterministically picking the farthest point?
+

A deterministic farthest-point rule would always select a single noisy outlier as a centroid with 100% certainty on every run, permanently wasting a cluster on it. Weighted-random selection still favors far points strongly but leaves room for legitimate structure to be discovered across multiple restarts.

Q15Medium
How does the Silhouette Score avoid the "always improves with more K" problem that inertia has?
+

Inertia only measures intra-cluster cohesion (a(i)), which mechanically improves as K grows. Silhouette also factors in inter-cluster separation (b(i)) — over-splitting a genuine cluster produces sub-clusters that sit close together, lowering b(i) and thus the score, so it peaks at a genuinely good K instead of monotonically improving.

Q16Medium
Why does K-Medians use the median instead of the mean, and what's the trade-off?
+

Minimizing Manhattan (L1) distance is optimized by the median, not the mean, since the derivative of |x−μ| is a constant sign, requiring equal counts on each side. K-Medians is more robust to outliers than K-Means, but the median has no closed-form multivariate solution, requiring iterative search rather than a simple average.

Q17Medium
Why does L2-normalizing vectors let you run cosine-based clustering using standard Euclidean K-Means?
+

For unit vectors, ‖x−μ‖² = 2(1−cosθ) — squared Euclidean distance is a constant multiple of cosine distance. Since scaling by a constant doesn't change the argmin ranking, Euclidean K-Means on normalized vectors produces identical cluster assignments to true cosine K-Means, while keeping the mean-based update mathematically valid.

Q18Medium
Describe a scenario with equal cluster sizes where K-Means still fails due to density alone.
+

Two clusters with the same number of points, but one tightly packed in a small radius and the other naturally spread over a much larger radius. K-Means penalizes the spread-out cluster's natural radius as if it "should" be tighter, sometimes splitting it unnecessarily — this is distinct from a size-imbalance failure, since both clusters here have equal point counts.

Q19Medium
What is the time complexity of K-Means per iteration, and which term dominates for high-dimensional sparse data?
+

O(N×K×D) per iteration. For high-dimensional data (large D) with modest N and small K, N and D jointly dominate; the standard fix is dimensionality reduction (PCA, or Truncated SVD for sparse data) before clustering.

Q20Medium
Why does Davies-Bouldin use the worst-case (max) neighboring cluster instead of averaging over all neighbors?
+

A clustering's quality is limited by its weakest link — one badly-overlapping neighbor is a real, visible defect. Averaging over many well-separated neighbors would dilute and hide that defect, producing an artificially good score. Using max makes the metric deliberately pessimistic, which is the point of a validation diagnostic.

Q21Hard
Prove that Lloyd's algorithm cannot cycle indefinitely between partitions.
+

J strictly decreases whenever the partition actually changes (both assignment and update steps are individually exact optimizations). If the algorithm cycled P₁→P₂→...→P₁, this would require J(P₁)>J(P₁) — a contradiction. Combined with only finitely many possible partitions of N points into K groups, the algorithm must reach a partition it cannot leave (convergence) within finite steps.

Q22Hard
Why is finding the globally optimal K-Means clustering NP-hard?
+

The number of possible partitions of N points into K groups is a Stirling number of the second kind, growing roughly as K^N/K! — combinatorially explosive. No known algorithm can search this space exactly in polynomial time as N grows, which is why Lloyd's greedy local-search heuristic is used instead of exhaustive search.

Q23Hard
Construct a concrete example where bad initialization traps Lloyd's algorithm in a genuinely wrong but stable local optimum.
+

Groups A={1,2,3,4}, B={5,6}, C={20,21}, K=3, init μ={1,2,20} (two centroids inside A). The algorithm converges to C1={1,2}, C2={3,4,5,6}, C3={20,21} — splitting A down the middle and merging its back half with all of B. No point wants to flip (stable), yet this isn't the natural human-obvious grouping — a genuine local optimum requires K≥3 with two close natural groups and one far group, unlike simple 2-cluster cases which almost always self-correct.

Q24Hard
Derive exactly what GMM reduces to when covariance is constrained to σ²I and E-step assignments are forced to be hard.
+

With Σ_k=σ²I for every cluster, the Gaussian's weighted distance (x−μ_k)ᵀΣ_k⁻¹(x−μ_k) collapses to (1/σ²)‖x−μ_k‖² — plain scaled Euclidean distance, identical in form for every cluster (removing per-cluster shape flexibility). Forcing E-step probabilities to hard 0/1 makes each point belong entirely to one cluster (removing soft ambiguity). Together, GMM's E-step/M-step become exactly Lloyd's assignment/update steps — K-Means is GMM under these two specific constraints.

Q25Hard
Why does an empty cluster arise during K-Means training, and how would you fix it in a from-scratch implementation?
+

An unlucky assignment step (often from bad init) can leave zero points assigned to some centroid — computing its mean then produces NaN and corrupts all subsequent iterations. Fixes: keep that centroid unchanged for the iteration, or re-seed it as the point currently farthest from all other centroids (a mini k-means++-style recovery), then continue.

Q26Hard
Why does standardization fix the feature-domination problem even without bounding values to a fixed range like min-max does?
+

Domination arises purely from unequal relative spread (variance) between features, not from unbounded ranges. Standardization (z=(x−μ)/σ) forces every feature's standard deviation to exactly 1, so a "1 std swing" in any feature contributes equally to squared Euclidean distance — no hard [0,1] boundary is required for this equalization to work.

Q27Hard
Explain the broadcasting mechanics behind a vectorized NumPy assignment step, including the intermediate array shape and its memory cost.
+

X[:,None,:] has shape (N,1,D); centroids[None,:,:] has shape (1,K,D). Broadcasting stretches both to (N,K,D), computing every point-centroid coordinate difference simultaneously. Summing over the D axis collapses this to the final (N,K) distance matrix. The intermediate (N,K,D) array costs O(NKD) memory — a real bottleneck at large N or D, which is why production libraries instead expand ‖x−μ‖²=‖x‖²+‖μ‖²−2x·μ using matrix multiplication for better memory efficiency.

Q28Hard
You run K-Means on 1M points with K=10 and D=50, then need to sweep K from 1 to 40 for an elbow plot. Walk through the computational implications and how you'd manage them.
+

Total cost is O(NKD×I) per K value; sweeping K from 1-40 instead of 1-10 roughly quadruples total computation for the search, since cost scales linearly in K. Practical mitigations: use Mini-Batch K-Means for the sweep to cut per-iteration cost via batching, cache/reuse k-means++ seeding logic where possible, and consider running the full sweep on a representative sample of the data rather than all 1M points, validating the chosen K on the full dataset afterward.

Q29Hard
A stakeholder asks why increasing K from 5 to 6 barely changed inertia but silhouette score dropped sharply. What's happening and what would you recommend?
+

Inertia barely changing signals K=6 isn't meaningfully reducing within-cluster distortion — the extra centroid is likely splitting a genuine cluster into two closely-spaced sub-clusters rather than capturing new real structure. Silhouette dropping confirms this: the split sub-clusters now sit close together, lowering inter-cluster separation (b(i)) for their points. Recommend reverting to K=5, since the elbow (near-flat inertia change) and quantitative silhouette both agree K=6 overfits the cluster count.

Q30Hard
Design a clustering approach for a dataset you suspect has non-convex clusters, mixed density, and no known K. Justify every choice.
+

1) Skip vanilla K-Means as the primary method — its spherical/equal-density assumption directly conflicts with the described structure. 2) Use DBSCAN or spectral clustering first, since both handle non-convex shapes and don't require a pre-specified K (DBSCAN infers cluster count from density; spectral clustering uses a similarity graph and eigen-decomposition to expose non-linear separability). 3) For density variation specifically, prefer DBSCAN's local density estimation (or GMM if clusters are elongated-but-Gaussian rather than arbitrarily shaped) over K-Means' single global distance criterion. 4) Validate with visual inspection (t-SNE/UMAP, aware that only local grouping is meaningful there) plus internal metrics appropriate to non-convex clusters (silhouette still applies; Davies-Bouldin/Calinski-Harabasz assume more centroid-like structure and are less reliable here). 5) Only fall back to K-Means if these methods reveal roughly convex, evenly-dense structure after all — using elbow+silhouette to pick K at that point.
